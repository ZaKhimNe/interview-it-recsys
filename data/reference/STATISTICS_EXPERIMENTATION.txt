## Descriptive Statistics

- **Mean**: The arithmetic average of a set of numbers, calculated as the sum of all values divided by the count of values. It is sensitive to outliers, which can skew the mean significantly.
  - **Mechanism**: \(\text{Mean} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i}{n}\)
  - **Practical Importance**: Useful for understanding the central tendency of a dataset. However, in skewed distributions, the mean may not accurately represent the data's center.

- **Median**: The middle value of a dataset when ordered from least to greatest. If the dataset has an even number of observations, the median is the average of the two middle numbers.
  - **Mechanism**: Sort the data and find the middle value.
  - **Practical Importance**: Provides a better measure of central tendency than the mean in skewed distributions or when outliers are present.

- **Mode**: The most frequently occurring value(s) in a dataset. A dataset may be unimodal, bimodal, or multimodal.
  - **Mechanism**: Count the frequency of each value and identify the highest.
  - **Practical Importance**: Useful for categorical data to identify the most common category.

- **Standard Deviation (std)**: A measure of the amount of variation or dispersion in a set of values. A low std indicates that the values tend to be close to the mean.
  - **Mechanism**: \(\text{std} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \text{mean})^2}{n}}\)
  - **Practical Importance**: Helps in understanding the spread of data points around the mean.

- **Variance**: The square of the standard deviation, representing the average of the squared differences from the mean.
  - **Mechanism**: \(\text{Variance} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \text{mean})^2}{n}\)
  - **Practical Importance**: Used in statistical tests and models to assess variability.

- **Skewness**: A measure of the asymmetry of the probability distribution of a real-valued random variable.
  - **Mechanism**: Positive skew indicates a longer or fatter tail on the right side, while negative skew indicates a longer or fatter tail on the left.
  - **Practical Importance**: Indicates potential outliers and the direction of skew, affecting mean and median.

## Probability Distributions

- **Normal Distribution**: A continuous probability distribution characterized by a symmetric, bell-shaped curve. Defined by its mean (\(\mu\)) and standard deviation (\(\sigma\)).
  - **Mechanism**: \(\text{PDF}(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}\)
  - **Practical Importance**: Many statistical tests assume normality due to the Central Limit Theorem.

- **Binomial Distribution**: A discrete distribution representing the number of successes in a fixed number of independent Bernoulli trials.
  - **Mechanism**: \(\text{P}(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}\)
  - **Practical Importance**: Models binary outcomes, such as pass/fail or yes/no.

- **Poisson Distribution**: A discrete distribution expressing the probability of a given number of events occurring in a fixed interval of time or space.
  - **Mechanism**: \(\text{P}(X = k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}\)
  - **Practical Importance**: Useful for modeling rare events.

## Hypothesis Testing

- **T-test**: Used to determine if there is a significant difference between the means of two groups.
  - **Mechanism**: Compares the t-statistic to a critical value from the t-distribution.
  - **Practical Importance**: Commonly used in A/B testing to compare group means.

- **Chi-square Test**: Tests the association between categorical variables.
  - **Mechanism**: Compares observed frequencies to expected frequencies under the null hypothesis.
  - **Practical Importance**: Useful for independence tests in contingency tables.

- **ANOVA (Analysis of Variance)**: Tests for significant differences among group means in a sample.
  - **Mechanism**: Compares the variance between groups to the variance within groups.
  - **Practical Importance**: Extends t-test to more than two groups.

## P-value Interpretation

- **P-value**: The probability of observing data as extreme as the observed data, assuming the null hypothesis is true.
  - **Mechanism**: Calculated using the test statistic and the relevant distribution.
  - **Practical Importance**: A p-value < 0.05 is commonly used as a threshold for statistical significance.
  - **Common Confusion**: Many candidates confuse p-value with the probability that the null hypothesis is true. The p-value does not measure this probability.

## Confidence Intervals

- **Confidence Interval (CI)**: A range of values derived from sample data that is likely to contain the true population parameter.
  - **Mechanism**: Calculated as \(\text{CI} = \text{mean} \pm \text{margin of error}\)
  - **Practical Importance**: Provides a range of plausible values for the parameter, reflecting the uncertainty of the estimate.

## A/B Testing Design and Pitfalls

- **Design**: Randomly assign participants to control and treatment groups to test the effect of a variable.
  - **Mechanism**: Use statistical tests to compare outcomes between groups.
  - **Practical Importance**: Helps in making data-driven decisions.
  - **Pitfalls**: Common issues include not accounting for multiple testing, insufficient sample size, and not randomizing properly.

## Statistical Significance vs Practical Significance

- **Statistical Significance**: Indicates that an observed effect is unlikely to have occurred by chance.
  - **Practical Significance**: Considers whether the effect size is large enough to be meaningful in a real-world context.
  - **Common Confusion**: Candidates often equate statistical significance with practical importance. A statistically significant result may not be practically significant.

## Multiple Testing Correction (Bonferroni)

- **Bonferroni Correction**: A method to address the problem of multiple comparisons by adjusting the significance threshold.
  - **Mechanism**: Divide the desired alpha level by the number of tests.
  - **Practical Importance**: Reduces the likelihood of Type I errors when conducting multiple hypothesis tests.
  - **Common Confusion**: Misunderstanding the need for correction when multiple tests increase the chance of false positives.

## Interviewer Red Flags

- Confusing **mean** with **median**: Candidates should understand that the mean is sensitive to outliers, while the median is not.
- Misinterpreting **p-value**: A candidate claiming that a p-value indicates the probability of the null hypothesis being true should be corrected.
- Ignoring **practical significance**: Candidates should not only focus on statistical significance but also consider the real-world impact of their findings.
