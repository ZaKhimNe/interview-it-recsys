## TIME_SERIES Competency: Data Scientist

This document outlines the core competencies and expected knowledge for a Data Scientist in the domain of Time Series analysis and forecasting. A strong candidate demonstrates not only theoretical understanding but also practical application, critical thinking, and an awareness of model limitations and business context.

## Components of a Time Series

A time series can typically be decomposed into several underlying components:

*   **Trend**: The long-term increase or decrease in the data. It represents the underlying direction of the series over time, independent of short-term fluctuations.
    *   *Distinction*: Differs from seasonality in its longer-term, non-periodic nature. A trend might be linear, exponential, or follow another functional form.
    *   *Interview Detail*: Strong candidates discuss how to model trends (e.g., linear regression, moving averages, splines) and the implications of additive vs. multiplicative trends (e.g., constant vs. increasing magnitude of change). Weak candidates might confuse trend with cyclic patterns or struggle to articulate its impact on forecasting.
*   **Seasonality**: A predictable, repeating pattern or cycle within a fixed period (e.g., daily, weekly, monthly, yearly). The length of the cycle is known and constant.
    *   *Distinction*: Differs from cyclic patterns, which are non-fixed in length and often irregular.
    *   *Interview Detail*: Strong candidates can identify seasonality from plots, discuss its period, and explain how it's handled in models (e.g., seasonal differencing, seasonal components in Holt-Winters, Fourier terms). They understand the difference between additive (constant seasonal amplitude) and multiplicative (seasonal amplitude proportional to level) seasonality.
*   **Cyclic**: Fluctuations that are not of fixed period, often longer than a year, and usually associated with economic or business cycles. They are less predictable in their timing and amplitude than seasonal patterns.
    *   *Distinction*: Key distinction from seasonality is the *non-fixed* period.
    *   *Interview Detail*: Strong candidates recognize that cyclic components are harder to model directly due to their irregular nature and are often absorbed into the trend or residual component in simpler models.
*   **Noise (Residuals)**: The irregular, random component of the time series that remains after accounting for trend, seasonality, and cyclic patterns. It represents unpredictable fluctuations.
    *   *Distinction*: It's what's left over; ideally, it should be white noise (zero mean, constant variance, no autocorrelation).
    *   *Interview Detail*: Strong candidates understand that residuals are crucial for model diagnostics. If residuals show patterns (e.g., autocorrelation), it indicates the model has not fully captured the underlying structure.

## Stationarity

A time series is **stationary** if its statistical properties (mean, variance, autocorrelation) do not change over time. This is a fundamental assumption for many traditional time series models like ARIMA.

*   **Importance**: Non-stationary series can lead to spurious regressions and unreliable forecasts.
*   **ADF (Augmented Dickey-Fuller) Test**:
    *   *Definition*: A statistical hypothesis test to determine if a unit root is present in a time series.
    *   *Null Hypothesis (H0)*: The time series has a unit root (is non-stationary).
    *   *Interpretation*: A low p-value (typically < 0.05) leads to rejection of H0, suggesting the series is stationary.
*   **KPSS Test**:
    *   *Definition*: A statistical hypothesis test for trend stationarity.
    *   *Null Hypothesis (H0)*: The time series is trend-stationary.
    *   *Interpretation*: A low p-value leads to rejection of H0, suggesting the series is non-stationary.
    *   *Distinction from ADF*: ADF tests for a unit root (difference stationarity), while KPSS tests for trend stationarity. They have opposite null hypotheses. A strong candidate knows to use both tests to confirm stationarity.
*   **Differencing to Achieve Stationarity**:
    *   *Definition*: Subtracting the previous observation from the current observation. First-order differencing removes a linear trend. Seasonal differencing (subtracting an observation from a previous season) removes seasonal patterns.
    *   *Interview Detail*: Strong candidates explain *why* differencing works (stabilizes the mean, removes trend/seasonality) and how to determine the appropriate order of differencing (e.g., by examining ACF/PACF plots or running stationarity tests on differenced data). Pitfall: Confusing the null hypotheses of ADF and KPSS, or applying differencing without justification.

## ARIMA Models

**ARIMA (AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average)** models are a class of statistical models for analyzing and forecasting time series data.

*   **Parameters (p, d, q)**:
    *   **p (AR order)**: The number of lag observations included in the model (autoregressive part).
        *   *Determination*: Often identified by the lag where the **PACF (Partial Autocorrelation Function)** plot cuts off or drops significantly.
    *   **d (Integrated order)**: The number of times the raw observations are differenced to make the time series stationary.
        *   *Determination*: Determined by the minimum differencing required for stationarity (e.g., from ADF/KPSS tests).
    *   **q (MA order)**: The number of lag forecast errors included in the model (moving average part).
        *   *Determination*: Often identified by the lag where the **ACF (Autocorrelation Function)** plot cuts off or drops significantly.
*   **ACF (Autocorrelation Function)**: Measures the correlation between a time series and its lagged values.
    *   *Interpretation*: Significant spikes at certain lags indicate correlation with past values. For MA models, ACF typically shows a sharp drop after lag q.
*   **PACF (Partial Autocorrelation Function)**: Measures the correlation between a time series and its lagged values, *after removing the effects of intermediate lags*.
    *   *Interpretation*: Significant spikes at certain lags indicate direct correlation. For AR models, PACF typically shows a sharp drop after lag p.
*   *Interview Detail*: Strong candidates can interpret ACF/PACF plots to suggest initial p and q values, understand the role of 'd' in achieving stationarity, and discuss the iterative process of model selection (e.g., using AIC/BIC). Pitfall: Misinterpreting ACF for AR order and PACF for MA order, or failing to explain the "partial" aspect of PACF.

## SARIMA Seasonal Parameters

**SARIMA (Seasonal AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average)** extends ARIMA to handle time series with seasonal components. It uses an additional set of seasonal parameters.

*   **Parameters (P, D, Q, m)**:
    *   **(P, D, Q)**: The seasonal equivalent of (p, d, q) for the seasonal part of the model.
        *   **P (Seasonal AR order)**: Number of seasonal lag observations.
        *   **D (Seasonal Integrated order)**: Number of seasonal differences.
        *   **Q (Seasonal MA order)**: Number of seasonal lag forecast errors.
    *   **m**: The number of observations per seasonal cycle (e.g., 12 for monthly data with yearly seasonality, 24 for hourly data with daily seasonality).
*   *Interview Detail*: Strong candidates explain how seasonal ACF/PACF plots (spikes at multiples of 'm') help determine P and Q, and how seasonal differencing (D) removes seasonal non-stationarity. They understand when SARIMA is appropriate and how it captures both short-term and long-term dependencies.

## Exponential Smoothing Models

Exponential smoothing models assign exponentially decreasing weights to past observations, giving more importance to recent data.

*   **Simple Exponential Smoothing (SES)**:
    *   *Definition*: Suitable for time series with no trend and no seasonality. It models only the level component.
    *   *Interview Detail*: Strong candidates understand its simplicity and use as a baseline for stable series.
*   **Holt's Linear Trend (Double Exponential Smoothing)**:
    *   *Definition*: Suitable for time series with a trend but no seasonality. It models both the level and trend components.
    *   *Interview Detail*: Strong candidates discuss the two smoothing parameters (for level and trend) and how they capture the evolving trend.
*   **Holt-Winters (Triple Exponential Smoothing)**:
    *   *Definition*: Suitable for time series with both trend and seasonality. It models level, trend, and seasonal components.
    *   *Types*: Can be additive (constant seasonal amplitude) or multiplicative (seasonal amplitude scales with level).
    *   *Interview Detail*: Strong candidates can explain the three smoothing parameters (level, trend, seasonality) and the choice between additive/multiplicative seasonality based on data characteristics. They recognize its robustness for many real-world series. Pitfall: Applying SES or Holt's to data with clear seasonality.

## Feature Engineering for Time Series

Creating relevant features from time series data is crucial for machine learning models.

*   **Lag Features**:
    *   *Definition*: Values of the series at previous time steps (e.g., `value_t-1`, `value_t-7`).
    *   *Purpose*: Capture autoregressive dependencies and provide the model with historical context.
    *   *Interview Detail*: Strong candidates discuss selecting relevant lags (e.g., based on ACF, domain knowledge) and the challenge of "cold start" for new series.
*   **Rolling Mean/Standard Deviation**:
    *   *Definition*: Statistical aggregates (mean, median, std dev, min, max) calculated over a moving window of past observations.
    *   *Purpose*: Smooth out noise, capture local trends, and measure volatility.
    *   *Interview Detail*: Strong candidates discuss the impact of window size on feature characteristics and how these features can capture short-term dynamics.
*   **Expanding Window Statistics**:
    *   *Definition*: Similar to rolling statistics, but the window expands to include all data from the start of the series up to the current point.
    *   *Purpose*: Capture cumulative trends or overall statistics.
    *   *Interview Detail*: Useful for capturing long-term growth or cumulative effects.
*   **Time-based Features**:
    *   *Definition*: Extracting components from the timestamp (e.g., day of week, month, quarter, year, hour, minute, holiday flags, business day indicator).
    *   *Purpose*: Capture cyclical patterns, seasonality, and known external events.
    *   *Interview Detail*: Strong candidates emphasize the importance of domain knowledge in selecting and creating these features (e.g., specific holidays, marketing events).

## Train/Test Split without Leakage & Cross-Validation for TS

Proper data splitting is paramount to avoid **data leakage** and obtain reliable model evaluation.

*   **Train/Test Split without Leakage**:
    *   *Definition*: The training set must always precede the test set chronologically. Future data must *never* be used to train or validate a model that predicts the future.
    *   *Importance*: Preserves the temporal order, simulating real-world forecasting.
    *   *Interview Detail*: Strong candidates explicitly state that random splitting is a critical pitfall for time series. They understand that any feature engineering (e.g., rolling means) must also respect this temporal order, computing features only from *past* data.
