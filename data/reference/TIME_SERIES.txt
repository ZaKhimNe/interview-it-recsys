## Components of Time Series

- **Trend**: A long-term increase or decrease in the data. It does not repeat and is not necessarily linear. Trends are important for understanding the overall direction of the data over time.
  - **Mechanism**: Identified using methods like moving averages or polynomial fitting.
  - **Practical Importance**: Helps in making long-term predictions and understanding the underlying growth or decline in the data.
  - **Common Confusion**: Trend vs. Seasonality. Trend is a long-term movement, while seasonality is a short-term repetitive pattern.

- **Seasonality**: Repeating patterns or cycles of behavior over a specific period, such as daily, monthly, or yearly.
  - **Mechanism**: Detected using Fourier transforms or seasonal decomposition.
  - **Practical Importance**: Critical for models that need to account for periodic fluctuations, such as retail sales.
  - **Common Confusion**: Seasonality vs. Cyclic. Seasonality is strictly periodic, while cyclic patterns are not.

- **Cyclic**: Fluctuations that are not of a fixed period, often influenced by economic or business cycles.
  - **Mechanism**: Identified through spectral analysis or business cycle indicators.
  - **Practical Importance**: Important for understanding economic indicators and business cycles.
  - **Common Confusion**: Cyclic vs. Seasonality. Cyclic patterns are irregular and not tied to a calendar.

- **Noise**: Random variation in the data that cannot be attributed to trend, seasonality, or cyclic patterns.
  - **Mechanism**: Often modeled as a white noise process.
  - **Practical Importance**: Noise reduction is crucial for improving model accuracy.
  - **Common Confusion**: Noise vs. Signal. Noise is the random error, while the signal is the meaningful information.

## Stationarity

- **Definition**: A time series is stationary if its statistical properties like mean, variance, and autocorrelation are constant over time.
  - **Mechanism**: Stationarity is tested using statistical tests like the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test and the Kwiatkowski-Phillips-Schmidt-Shin (KPSS) test.
  - **Practical Importance**: Many time series models assume stationarity, making it a prerequisite for model fitting.

- **ADF Test**: Tests the null hypothesis that a unit root is present in a time series sample.
  - **Threshold**: p-value < 0.05 typically indicates stationarity.
  - **Common Confusion**: ADF vs. KPSS. ADF tests for a unit root (non-stationarity), while KPSS tests for stationarity.

- **KPSS Test**: Tests the null hypothesis that a time series is stationary around a deterministic trend.
  - **Threshold**: p-value < 0.05 typically indicates non-stationarity.
  - **Common Confusion**: KPSS vs. ADF. KPSS is often used to confirm ADF results.

- **Differencing**: A technique to achieve stationarity by subtracting the previous observation from the current observation.
  - **Mechanism**: First-order differencing is often sufficient, but higher orders may be necessary.
  - **Practical Importance**: Essential for transforming non-stationary data into a stationary form for modeling.

## ARIMA Models

- **Definition**: Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) models are used for forecasting stationary time series data.
  - **Parameters**: 
    - **p**: Autoregressive order.
    - **d**: Degree of differencing.
    - **q**: Moving average order.
  - **Mechanism**: Combines autoregressive and moving average components with differencing to model time series data.
  - **Practical Importance**: Widely used for its flexibility in modeling various types of time series data.

- **ACF/PACF Interpretation**:
  - **ACF (Autocorrelation Function)**: Measures the correlation between observations at different lags.
  - **PACF (Partial Autocorrelation Function)**: Measures the correlation between observations at different lags after removing the effects of shorter lags.
  - **Practical Importance**: Used to identify the order of AR and MA components in ARIMA models.

## SARIMA Models

- **Definition**: Seasonal ARIMA models extend ARIMA by including seasonal components.
  - **Seasonal Parameters**:
    - **P**: Seasonal autoregressive order.
    - **D**: Seasonal differencing order.
    - **Q**: Seasonal moving average order.
    - **s**: Length of the seasonal cycle.
  - **Practical Importance**: Essential for modeling time series data with seasonal patterns.

## Exponential Smoothing

- **Simple Exponential Smoothing**: Suitable for data without trend or seasonality.
  - **Mechanism**: Weighted average of past observations with exponentially decreasing weights.
  - **Practical Importance**: Useful for short-term forecasting.

- **Holt’s Linear Trend Model**: Extends simple exponential smoothing to capture linear trends.
  - **Mechanism**: Adds a trend component to the smoothing equation.
  - **Practical Importance**: Suitable for data with a trend but no seasonality.

- **Holt-Winters Seasonal Model**: Extends Holt’s model to capture seasonality.
  - **Mechanism**: Adds seasonal components to the trend and level equations.
  - **Practical Importance**: Suitable for data with both trend and seasonality.

## Feature Engineering for Time Series

- **Lag Features**: Use past observations as features for forecasting.
  - **Practical Importance**: Captures temporal dependencies in the data.

- **Rolling Mean/Std**: Calculate moving averages or standard deviations over a window.
  - **Practical Importance**: Smooths out short-term fluctuations and highlights longer-term trends.

- **Expanding Window**: Uses all available data up to the current point for calculations.
  - **Practical Importance**: Captures cumulative effects over time.

## Train/Test Split Without Leakage

- **Definition**: Splitting data into training and testing sets while preserving the temporal order.
  - **Mechanism**: Ensures that future data is not used in training, preventing data leakage.
  - **Practical Importance**: Critical for maintaining the integrity of model evaluation.

## Cross-Validation for Time Series

- **Time-Series Split**: A method of cross-validation that respects the temporal order of data.
  - **Mechanism**: Splits data into training and testing sets in a rolling or expanding window fashion.
  - **Practical Importance**: Provides a more reliable estimate of model performance on unseen data.

## Forecasting Evaluation

- **MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error)**: Measures the accuracy of a forecast as a percentage.
  - **Formula**: \( \text{MAPE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum \left| \frac{A_t - F_t}{A_t} \right| \times 100 \)
  - **Practical Importance**: Easy to interpret and widely used.

- **SMAPE (Symmetric Mean Absolute Percentage Error)**: A variation of MAPE that is symmetric.
  - **Formula**: \( \text{SMAPE} = \frac{100\%}{n} \sum \frac{|F_t - A_t|}{(|A_t| + |F_t|)/2} \)
  - **Practical Importance**: Addresses the asymmetry issue in MAPE.

- **MAE (Mean Absolute Error)**: Measures the average magnitude of errors in a set of forecasts.
  - **Formula**: \( \text{MAE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum |A_t - F_t| \)
  - **Practical Importance**: Provides a straightforward measure of forecast accuracy.

- **RMSE (Root Mean Square Error)**: Measures the square root of the average squared differences between forecasted and observed values.
  - **Formula**: \( \text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum (A_t - F_t)^2} \)
  - **Practical Importance**: Penalizes larger errors more than MAE, making it sensitive to outliers.

## Interviewer Red Flags

- Confusing trend with seasonality or cyclic patterns.
- Misinterpreting ADF and KPSS test results.
- Incorrectly setting ARIMA parameters without justifying the choice based on ACF/PACF analysis.
- Ignoring the importance of train/test split without leakage in time series data.
- Failing to use appropriate cross-validation techniques for time series.
